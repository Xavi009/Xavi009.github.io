1. What is this?

In 2019, Bearospace @ UCLA achieved first place in NASA's First Nations Launch (FNL). Following this success, Bearospace applied and was accepted to NASA Student Launch (formerly known as University Student Launch Initiative, or USLI). USLI is a research-based, competitive, high-powered rocketry competition focused primarily on developing unique payload systems. It strives to provide, relevant, cost-effective research and development of current objectives/problems faced by the Artemis program. Unlike FNL, USLI is not limited to minorities, nor does it provide funding for teams. USLI is a more competitive and higher caliber student project. I encourage you to read the Context & Struggles section to better understand the scope and effort USLI meant for the Bearospace team.

1.1. Relevant Requirements

This year's unique payload criteria was to design a system capable of being launched in a high power rocket, landing safely, and recovering simulated lunar ice from one of several locations on the surface of the launch field. Important high-level payload design requirements are presented below:

  1. Five recovery areas will be located on the surface of the launch field. Teams may recover a sample from any of the recovery areas. Each recovery site will be at least 3 feet in diameter and contain sample material extending from ground level to at least 2 inches below the surface.
  2. The recovered ice sample will be a minimum of 10 milliliters (mL).
  3. Once the sample is recovered, it must be stored and transported at least 10 linear feet from the recovery area.
  4. Teams must abide by all FAA and NAR rules and regulations.
  5. Black Powder and/or similar energetics are only permitted for deployment of in-flight recovery systems. Any ground deployments must utilize mechanical systems.
  6. Any part of the payload or vehicle that is designed to be deployed, whether on the ground or in the air, must be fully retained until it is deployed as designed.

Additional general, vehicle, recovery, and safety requirements are outlined in the manual but are not as applicable to my role so they will not be discussed.

1.2. Grading

Grading for the competition was based on major milestone documents and deliverables in addition to physical launch vehicle objectives. Milestone deliverables (first 4 bullet points) include a report, presentation, and milestone proof of concept launches if applicable.

  • Preliminary Design Review (PDR) (20%)
  • Critical Design Review (CDR) (20%)
  • Flight Readiness Review (FRR) (20%)
  • Post Launch Assessment Review (PLAR) (15%)
  • Flight and Recovery (10%)
  • Altitude (10%)
  • STEM Engagement (5%)

Due to COVID-19, grading criteria that depended on physically building and launching the rocket were omitted from the competition.

2. My Roles & Tasks

Because of the extreme lack of members between the previous year and this year, I was formally known as both the electronics lead and the payloads lead. To some degree, this dual role made it easier to collaborate between the electronics and the payloads team since I was essentially both teams, but the workload was still twice as much. 2.1. Payload

As the payloads lead, I was responsible for designing, manufacturing, and mechanically testing the payload and its associated deployment method. This meant a lot of SolidWorks, 3D printing, CNC milling and modifying commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) components. Each dimension was iteratively modified during the R&D phase of manufacturing. As the only member of the payloads team, I spent almost all my time in the machine shop, either starting 3D prints or CNC milling wooden components.

The payload design features two functional components:

  1. The Rover Ejection Assembly (REA)
  2. The Rover

The rover ejection assembly is tasked with retaining and deploying the rover in the correct orientation after a landing event has been identified. Once deployed, the payload will begin radio frequency communication with a driver on the ground. The rover will receive inputs from the driver and return video feedback to the driver to complete the mission.

2.1.1. Rover Ejection Assembly (REA)

Using the above figure as reference, the leftmost stepper motor (1) is attached to a bulkhead (3) using epoxy. This stepper motor connects to the entire assembly to rotate the payload. Attaching the motor to the entire assembly makes the motor a load bearing component. A large moment will be induced on the stepper motor from the protruding rods and payload resting on the rods. To prevent this, an additional aluminum alloy bearing ring (4) will be used. The bearing ring will support the entire assembly so that the leftmost stepper motor will only rotate the assembly, not support it. All load bearing forces will be applied to the bearing ring. Since the purchased bearing ring is flat, additional 3D printed rings (2 & 6) are placed to offset one rotating ring from the other. These standoff rings interface with the bearing ring and the bulkhead or circular block holder (7). A circular tube (5) will fit around the shaft of the stepper motor and will be epoxied into the large circular block holder (7). The circular block holder acts as a rotating bulkhead for which to attach the rods and additional stepper motor onto. Cutouts for another stepper motor, a gyroscope/accelerometer module, and a steel rod are provided. The unthreaded rod (9) and stepper motor (1) will be epoxied into the designated hole inside the circular block holder. In order to attach the lead screw (10) onto the stepper motor, a 5mm to 10mm coupling (9) will be used. The coupling allows two shafts of different diameters to be attached by adjusting the force applied on each using screws. Both the lead screw and unthreaded rod are ⅜" in diameter to provide enough rigidity to support the weight of the payload.

A unique aspect of the rover deployment mechanism is the circular block holder (7). Dimensions of each cut were dictated by the size of the stepper motor, interference with the eyebolt attached to the bulkhead, and size constraints imposed by the rover ebay. A concern with the shape of the circular block holder was its thin wall size for housing the stepper motor compromising the overall strength of the retention system and how protrusion of the stepper motor outside the boundary of the circular block holder outer diameter clashes with the eyebolt if driven the wrong way. The following figure demonstrates the reasoning for the circular block holder.

The diameter of the circular block holder allots space for the leftmost eyebolt (which is used for the launch vehicle's parachute), however, this means part of the stepper motor is exposed. If rotated counterclockwise, the stepper motor will interfere with the eyebolt, which jeopardizes the deployment mechanism’s ability to orient the rover. Moving the motor and shaft towards the center would mitigate this problem, but would limit the space available for electronics onboard the rover.

2.1.2. Rover

The payload features a 11.4" x 4.3" x 4.2" rover with a collapsible collection arm and antenna for storage within the launch vehicle. A 2.3" x 1.9" x 0.4" (~28.6mL) storage area within the chassis of the rover will store simulated lunar ice samples. The same collection arm doubles as a cap for the collection bin to ensure collected samples are retained within the rover during transportation. A separate 2.4" x 3.0" x 4.0" compartmentalized section, located on the back side of the rover, is dedicated to housing all onboard electronics. A requirement in the USLI handbook states that all lithium polymer batteries must be sufficiently protected and marked as flammable. To satisfy this requirement, the single lithium polymer battery powering onboard electronics is located in a separate, bright red compartment within the electronics bay. All other electronics are placed on the top half of the electronics bay for easier access to electronics. Both compartments and lid are fastened to each other using 6-32 nuts and bolts. The bottom red compartment of the ebay is attached to the chassis using epoxy. The contact area between the chassis and the bottom ebay compartment is maximized to increase the amount of epoxy applied between the two parts. The rover uses wheels instead of treads as its form of movement. While this limits the rover’s ability to overcome obstacles located under its center, the driver will take extra precaution in avoiding obstacles.

A crucial component for deploying the rover and managing electronics onboard the rover is the electronics bay (7 & 8). Since the electronics bay interfaces with the deployment mechanism’s rods, the electronics bay must support the weight of the rover and landing impact forces while stored within the launch vehicle. A custom printed female threaded rod (10) interfaces with the threaded rod of the REA. A small camera is mounted on top of the payload on component (9) for video feedback. Additional design considerations were critical for all components but will be omitted for brevity.

2.2.1. REA

In order to orient and deploy the rover after launch, the rover ejection assembly employs a series of electronics to autonomously accomplish this. This bypasses the challenge of establishing a fickle surface RF communication between the team and the launch vehicle and removes the need of additional antennas. The individual components are discussed in the following sections.

Component Purpose Quantity
Arduino Uno Microcontroller. Reads data from pressure sensor and accelerometer/gyroscope modules and control stepper motor driver. 1
BMP180 Barometric pressure sensor (BMP). Sends pressure data to microcontroller to measure current altitude of the launch vehicle. 1
MPU-6050 Accelerometer/gyroscope sensor (6DOF). Records translational acceleration and rotational velocity. Used to provide positional feedback of REA’s rotating block. 1
A4988 Stepper motor driver module. Regulates current supplied to stepper motors and simplify wiring and coding of Arduino with stepper motor. 2
NEMA 17 Stepper Motor Stepper motor. Rotates REA rotating block and lead screw with high torque and precision. 2
3S 700mAh LiPo Power supply for stepper motors. Stepper motors require high voltage and discharge rates to operate, all which LiPos can do. 1
9V Alkaline Battery Logic power supply for Arduino Uno and its peripherals. 1

Since the rover must be autonomously deployed, the Arduino microcontroller must be able to identify when a touchdown event has occurred after launch. The BMP and accelerometer/gyroscope modules help the Arduino Uno decide when to deploy. The following flow chart illustrates the steps taken by the Arduino Uno for safe deployment.

Calibration

Apogee

Landing

Orient

Deploy

  • Calibration: once power is supplied outside the launch vehicle at the launch pad, the Arduino will take averaged altitude readings from the BMP to determine its current altitude on the launch pad. A touch down event will be determined based on this initial pressure reading.
  • Apogee: the Arduino code will define apogee as the highest point after reaching an arming altitude of 1000 ft higher than the calibrated ground level altitude. This allows the Arduino to differentiate between when the launch vehicle is on the pad and when it has landed.
  • Landing once altitude values stop fluctuating during descent, a landing has occured. Current altitude will be compared with calibrated altitude to verify the launch vehicle has landed. A two minute delay will initiate prior to the next step.
  • Orient after the delay, angular position and gravitional acceleraion of the rover will be analyzed using the accelerometer and gyroscope module imbedded in the rotating block. A stepper motor will drive the REA until the correct orientation is achieved.
  • Deploy: a second stepper motor will drive the REA’s linear actuator to deploy the rover.

2.2.2. Rover

Rover electronics were selected from commercially available drone parts for their small form factor, compatibility with well known components, and high-powered RF capabilities. An Arduino Uno is much larger than the selected flight controller used which made this option an attractive one. This ended up being a huge mistake as the team was not able to program the system to better fit the team’s needs. A better solution would be to use dual in-line package (DIP) chip microcontrollers and wire all necessary peripherals and components to a perfboard. This would allow greater control over the designed system in terms of hardware and software while remaining relatively small enough to fit within the payload.

2.2.3. Verification & Testing

One of the main concerns of the rover electronics was establishing RF connection through an impermeable surface, such as carbon fiber from the body tube, that could reach a range of 0.5 miles (launch field is 2500 ft in radius). Prior to testing the Tx/Rx and VTx/VRx pairs, the ability of the selected electronics to accomplish a minimum 0.5 mile radius was verified using the Friis Transmission Equation shown below:

$$P_R= {P_T\ G_T\ G_R\ c^2} \over {(4\pi Rf)}^2 $$

Where PR is the received power in W, PT is the power of the transmitter in W, GT and GR are the antenna gains of the transmitter and receiver respectively in dBi, c is the speed of light in m/s, R is the antenna separation in m, and f is the frequency in Hz. This equation assumes both antennas are in free space (i.e. no obstructions nearby).

For the following calculations, both the transmitter and receiver will use the same TrueRC Singularity RHCP 2.4GHz antenna with a gain of 1.6dBi. Since the antennas are all the same polarization (RHCP), there is no need to consider the polarization loss factor. The desired radius is 0.5 miles which is ~804.7 meters. The sensitivity of each receiver is the minimum gain needed to fully receive a signal. A gain higher than that of the sensitivity indicates a successful transmission. Relevant transmitter and receiver parameters are tabulated below. Unit converions were made outside of the scope of the formula.

Tx/Rx 0.1 1.6 1.6 -104 -74.94

Another verification step taken was a battery run-time analysis and current draw bottleneck analysis. Many of the electrical components passively draw small amounts of current while idle. During launch day, it is expected for a launch vehicle to be on the launch pad for no more than 2 hours. Since the launch vehicle design did not include an external switch to power the rover specifically, the team aimed to have a battery run-time of 4 hours for idling and 30 mins for completing the mission. The math for this is simple: current draw of components (mA) multiplied by the number of hours running (h) and compare the (mAh) needed vs how much the battery can supply. The following figure tabulates current draws of electrical components of the rover. Using the mentioned 11.1V 3S 4000 mAh 50C LiPo battery, the battery exceeds the teams 4 hour and 30 minute idle and mission time requirement as this only draws up to 3485 mAh and the battery can supply 4000 mAh. The battery also supplies the appropriate voltages to high voltage components while the XT60 PDB steps down voltage for all other 5V logic components.

Another important thing to note is the max current draw of the DC motors is up to 10.6A. While full throttle will never happen, it is important to consider how the system would react if this unlikely case were to occur. Each DC motor can draw up to 10.6A for a total current draw of 42.4A. The selected ESC is rated for 25A each DC motor, so no bottlenecking occurs. The LiPo battery selected is rated 50C at 4000 mAh, or 4 Ah. This means the LiPo battery can safely discharge up to 200A at once. The current draw of all electrical components at their max limit does not exceed the max discharge rate of the battery, so the system is more than capable for delivering all required voltages and currents.
