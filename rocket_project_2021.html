1. What is this?

In the 2020-2021 school year, Bearospace @ UCLA applied and was accepted to NASA Student Launch (formerly known as University Student Launch Initiative, or USLI). USLI is a research-based, competitive, high-powered rocketry competition focused primarily on developing unique payload systems. It strives to provide relevant, cost-effective research and development of current objectives/problems faced by the Artemis program.

This year, the competition was divided into two divisions due to COVID-19: a Launch division and a Design Only division. Due to restrictions at UCLA and student members taking classes remotely, Bearospace participated in the Design Only division. Please look at the Context & Struggles section to understand the current leads' objectives for this year.

1.1. Relevant Requirements

This year's unique payload criteria was to design a lander capable of being launched in a high power rocket, deployed from the launch vehicle during its descent phase, landing safely, taking a 360-degree panoramic photo, and transmitting the picture to the team. Important high-level payload design requirements are presented below:

  1. The landing system will be completely jettisoned from the rocket at an altitude between 500 and 1,000 ft. AGL and must not be tethered to the launch vehicle.
  2. The landing system will land in an upright orientation or will be capable of reorienting itself to an upright configuration after landing. The system will self-level to within a five-degree tolerance from vertical.
  3. Upon completion of reorientation and self-levelling, the lander will produce a 360-degree panoramic image of the landing site and transmit it to the team.
  4. Teams must abide by all FAA and NAR rules and regulations.
  5. Black Powder and/or similar energetics are only permitted for deployment of in-flight recovery systems. Any ground deployments must utilize mechanical systems.
  6. Any part of the payload or vehicle that is designed to be deployed, whether on the ground or in the air, must be fully retained until it is deployed as designed.

Additional general, vehicle, recovery, and safety requirements are outlined in the manual but are not as applicable to my role so they will not be discussed.

1.2. Grading

Grading for the Design Only division was based on major milestone documents and deliverables, in addition to a Payload Modification Vehicle Redesign (PMVR) unique to the Design Only division. Milestone deliverables (first 4 bullet points) include a report and a presentation. No milestone launches were required due to COVID-19.

  • Preliminary Design Review (PDR) (15%)
  • Critical Design Review (CDR) (25%)
  • Flight Readiness Review (FRR) (30%)
  • Payload Modification Vehicle Redesign (15%)
  • STEM Engagement (5%)

The grading breakdown adds up to 90% instead of 100% because the Launch Division includes an additional 10% for target altitude accuracy.

2. My Roles & Tasks

This year I was only the payloads lead as Bearospace achieved member retention from the previous year. I had a dedicated team that I was able to leverage to help design and write reports related to the payload. Since this was my last year at Bearospace, I was primarily focused on teaching and preparing future members and leads on the iterative process of designing a payload.

2.1. Payload

As the payloads lead, I was responsible for designing and virtually testing the payload and its associated deployment/retention system. This meant a lot of SolidWorks, maintaining an exhaustive BOM, and virtually stress testing mission-critical components using SolidWorks FEA tools.

The payload design features two functional components:

  1. The Lander
  2. The Retention System Assembly

The rover ejection assembly is tasked with retaining and deploying the rover in the correct orientation after a landing event has been identified. Once deployed, the payload will begin radio frequency communication with a driver on the ground. The rover will receive inputs from the driver and return video feedback to the driver to complete the mission.

2.1.1. Lander

The payload assembly is a 6.1" tall by 4.4" diameter 3-legged lander that houses electronics necessary for completing the mission within its body. Each leg is individually controlled by a singular servo motor. While within the launch vehicle, the payload's legs are packed in an upright configuration to minimize the effective volume of the payload. During descent, the legs will be actuated to be parallel with the base of the payload to effectively increase the surface area of the payload for landing. Once a landing event is detected, the payload will orient itself within 5° of vertical by rotating the legs and obtaining feedback from a gyroscope/accelerometer module. A central column perches the camera for a greater field of view of the landing site. The camera is mounted on a continuous servo motor to rotate the camera 360°. In order to center the camera within the payload and provide the camera with a clear view of the landing site for taking pictures, acrylic sheets are used. The main cover is attached to the base of the payload using six pairs of M2.5 screws and M2.5 threaded heat set inserts which are implemented in the base component for a strong and detachable interface. The use of screws and heat set inserts allows the team to easily access and troubleshoot electronics within the payload. Electronics within the payload are harnessed using pairs of M2.5 screws and threaded heat set inserts or are compartmentalized within the base component of the payload.

2.1.2. Retention System Assembly

The retention system used involves a cage-like mechanism that houses the payload from within. The retention system is tethered to the launch vehicle's main parachute shock cord using two 3/8" galvanized steel eyebolts and is deployed during the vehicle's main parachute's deployment at 700 ft AGL. The bottom of the retention system is tethered to the launch vehicle's bulkhead and the other side is tethered to the main parachute using separate shock cords. The 30" length shock cord between the retention system and the launch vehicle's bulkhead allows the payload to exit the 16" long body tube and jettison the payload without colliding with the launch vehicle. An added shield component is packed upright within the launch vehicle to prevent the payload from colliding and damaging itself with the inner wall of the upper body tube during deployment. The shield is tethered to the bottom-most eyebolt as shown above to prevent the component from detaching from the launch vehicle.

Additional 3D printed circular components create a flat surface on which the payload can rest on and prevent the payload’s parachute from entangling with the retention system. A green semicircular enclosure prevents the payload and its parachute from deploying on sides opposite of each other during its ejection.

2.2. Locking Mechanism & Avionics Sled

A miscellaneous component I was in charge of was devising a secure and repeatable way to place electronics within the launch vehicle without compromising the structural integrity of the vehicle's safety hardware. The devised solution is a slide-and-lock locking mechanism that also holds the avionics sled. This allows the team to make all necessary wiring from outside the launch vehicle and then lock the entire system into place without damaging the launch vehicle using screws like other teams.

The locking mechanism is comprised of an inner and outer ring. The inner and outer rings are made from .75" thick pine wood planks and reinforced with .12" thick aluminum sheets. This year, the avionics sled was setup differently. Instead of attaching the sled to the inner ring of the locking mechanism, the sled slides into two threaded rods attached to a bulkhead. Because of the load-bearing importance of the bulkhead, this bulkhead is also reinforced with a .12" thick aluminum sheet. The avionics sled is held in place with lock nuts using the threaded rods. Heat-set inserts are welded into the plastic sled so that electronics can be screwed onto the sled using M2.5 screws, as shown below.

2.3. Virtual Testing

Because of the virtual nature of this year's competition, all stress testing was done using SolidWorks FEA with no physical testing for verification. Since I was in charge of both the locking mechanism and payload, 3 load-bearing components were identified for these sub-systems.

2.3.1. Locking Mechanism

The locking mechanism must endure shock forces experienced during the launch vehicle's drogue chute deployment. Since this assembly is related to the safety of the launch vehicle, a factor of safety of 4 was applied to the expected force for all testing.

For the inner locking mechanism, the 3 tab sections were grounded during FEA analysis and a force was applied on the underside of the system. This force was limitied to the area of a hex nut. A similar approach was taken for the outer ring. The outer diameter of the ring was grounded and 3 forces totalling the expected force were applied on the underside of the top ring. These forces were limited to the area of the tabs of the inner ring.

Component Expected Force (lbf) Factor of Safety Force (x4) (lbf) Max von Mises Stress (psi) Yield Strength of 6061 Aluminum (psi)
Inner Ring 18.69 74.79 1762 40000
Outer Ring 18.69 74.79 68.75

2.3.2. Retention System Assembly

Since the Retention System Assembly is connected between the main parachute and the launch vehicle via the main shock cord, the Assembly is also a mission critical component related to the overall safety of the launch vehicle. Like the locking mechanism, the Retention System Assembly components are also tested with a factor of safety of 4. However, the expected force is different since at this point the rocket already deployed its drogue chute and will deploy its main chute.

Two load-bearing components were identified on the Retention System Assembly: (1) the 3/8" threaded rods running across the assembly (2) the pine and aluminum bulkheads attached to the eyebolts.

(1) To reduce computation time, the 3/8" threaded rods were modelled as a simple cylinder with a 3/8" diameter. Half the testing load was applied to a single rod as there are two rods in the assembly. (2) A similar approach to the locking mechanism was implemented to the bulkhead ends of the assembly. The applied force was limited to the area of the lock nut on the eyebolt. The bulkhead was grounded at two, lock nut shaped areas where the bulkhead interfaces with the 3/8" threaded rods.

Component Expected Force (lbf) Factor of Safety Force (x4) (lbf) Max von Mises Stress (psi) Yield Strength (psi)
3/8" Threaded Rod 20.30 81.20 193.2 36000 (alloy steel)
Bulkhead 10.15 40.60 96.70 40000 (aluminum)

2.3.3. Lander

The payload itself also contains a parachute system. As such, it is important to test the load-bearing capabilities of the payload to ensure human and mission safety. The top cap that contains an eyelet for the payload's parachute was idenfitied as a point of concern. A simulation force was applied on the inner area of the eyelet/loop with an "upwards" vector. The inner sections where the acrylic sheets are glued were grounded.

Component Expected Force (lbf) Factor of Safety Force (x4) (lbf) Max von Mises Stress (psi) Yield Strength of ABS (psi)
Payload Top Cap 0.90 3.62 68.79 7000

2.4. Reports & Presentations

A huge component of being a technical lead is writing reports detailing the progress of the project. As the payloads, I was in charge of writing the technical details of all payload and locking mechanism structural components, creating engineering drawings, build instructions, and test plans, and verifying requirements compliance within each relevant subsection. In addition to reports, presentations were created and presented to NASA engineers for 45 min with a 15 min Q&A.

A lot of focus was placed on giving continuing members more autonomy and experience for the following year when they become leads. Member participation for creating technical drawings, creating build instructions, and writing about these components was a great sign for the longetivity of Bearospace.
